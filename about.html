About this Settlement Explorer

Settlement-path observability for disputed Polymarket-UMA contracts — a public-facing companion to the working paper "Settlement-State Construction in Prediction-Market Contracts."

The argument in one paragraph

Prediction-market prices are usually interpreted as beliefs about events. But payoffs depend on a contractual mapping from world facts to a settlement label. This project distinguishes three objects: realized world facts W, the rule-implied label G under the contract's text and source hierarchy, and the settlement state S delivered by the mechanism. The paper reconstructs this mapping for disputed Polymarket contracts resolved through UMA. The Settlement Explorer is the public version of that reconstruction: where does a given disputed contract sit in the funnel, and how did its final payoff get produced?

Headline facts

  • 661,949 total settlement conditions in the Polymarket-via-UMA panel.
  • 2,221 are disputed (at least one counter-bond posted).
  • 2,197 disputed contracts have an observed final payoff state — the analysis sample.
  • 530, or 24.1%, end with a final payoff different from the first proposed outcome.
  • Of these revisions, 352 (66%) occur through repeated adapter-routed requests; 178 (34%) occur via DVM request-voiding.
  • A two-pass LLM-coded benchmark identifies 94 candidate mismatches (final payoff vs rule-implied label), of which 64 are agreed across both coding passes. This is a diagnostic layer, not an externally validated error count.

Scope & non-claims

This project is a measurement project. It reconstructs and reports the path from first proposal to final payoff for disputed contracts. It does not:

  • Identify the unconditional rate at which a randomly selected market receives an erroneous final payoff.
  • Adjudicate whether any specific contract's final payoff is correct in real-world terms.
  • Study UMA tokenholder voter identity, vote concentration, or turnout. Those questions live elsewhere.
  • Estimate causal effects of any UMA policy change.

When you click through to a contract, you see how its final payoff was produced — not whether it's "right."

Data sources

Polymarket markets and UMA Optimistic Oracle resolution events are ingested into a research database. The settlement panel covers all Polymarket conditions resolved via UMA. The disputed-contract sample (D=1) is the union of contracts with at least one counter-bond posted. Episode-level chains are reconstructed from the UMA RequestPrice / DisputePrice / SettlePrice event sequence.

The candidate-mismatch layer uses a two-pass LLM coding protocol against the recoverable contract text and named source. Coverage and limitations are documented in the paper.

Limitations

Several caveats apply to the current export:

  • Question-text coverage is 79.1%. 1,756 of 2,221 disputed contracts have recoverable question text in pm_markets.question, episode_panel.question_text, or pm_resolution_events.ancillary_data_decoded. The other 465 are visible in the rev3 settlement panel but absent from the Polymarket-side metadata (likely Kalshi-only or deprecated condition_ids). The Contract Explorer shows [question text missing] for affected rows; chain type and final payoff are still observed.
  • The Settlement Auditability checklist uses heuristic regex over question text + contract description + ancillary data. Items 1–4 (rule text / named source / fallback / edge cases) are noisy: they tag whether the relevant language appears, not whether the contract was correctly designed. Items 5–8 (chain completeness / repeated-request / final payoff / benchmark agreement) are derived from the on-chain settlement data and are deterministic.
  • The Public Case Locator uses keyword matching on pm_markets.question. It locates candidate markets in the panel but does not decide which specific contract the public controversy referred to. Three of seven curated cases (Venezuela / Iran-Khamenei / Lebanon) match in the market panel but not in the disputed-analysis sample — this could mean the relevant contract was excluded under the paper's sample-construction rules, or that the public dispute attached to a Kalshi-side market not visible here.

Reading the paper

The full working paper, "Settlement-State Construction in Prediction-Market Contracts," contains the formal estimands, sample-construction details, robustness checks, and full case appendices. The dashboard is intentionally a thinner artifact — you can see the funnel and chain mechanics here, but the academic argument lives in the paper.
