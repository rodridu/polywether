Cite check · for journalists

Should you cite this prediction-market price?

Reporters often write "the prediction market puts the chance at X%." That number is only as meaningful as the contract behind it. This page checks whether a market's contract is clean enough that the price means what readers will think it means.

Two layers: price robustness (is the price liquid enough to cite?) and settlement robustness (is the contract clear enough for the price to mean what readers assume?). We answer the second. For the first, see Bellwether.

The 6-question check

Before quoting a prediction-market probability in a story, run a market through these six questions. We pull what we can from the panel; you fill in the rest.

  1. ① Is the resolution rule recoverable?

    Question text + description + UMA ancillary data, ideally all three. If only the question is recoverable, the contract may resolve under terms readers can't see.

    From the panel: 79.1% of disputed contracts have any recoverable rule text. The other 21% are essentially un-auditable from the buyer side.

  2. ② Does the rule name an external source?

    "Resolves according to {ESPN / OMB / Reuters / official certification}". Without a named source, the resolution depends on whoever is asked.

    From the panel: contracts with rule text but no named source revise 45% of the time among disputed cases. With a named source, the rate drops to 18%.

  3. ③ Does the rule specify a fallback?

    What happens if the primary source is unavailable, delayed, or itself disputed? "If the official source has not announced by {date}, AP wire confirmation suffices."

    From the panel: only 49% of disputed contracts have any detectable fallback language.

  4. ④ Does the rule handle edge cases?

    Cancellation, postponement, ties, partial events, time-zone ambiguity. Edge-case silence shifts decisions to the oracle ex post.

    From the panel: only 24% of disputed contracts have detectable edge-case language.

  5. ⑤ Has this contract already been disputed or revised?

    If the chain shows a prior dispute, repeated adapter-routed request, or DVM voiding, the price may already reflect settlement-risk discounting rather than pure event probability.

    From the panel: 24.1% of disputed contracts revise from first proposal to final payoff. Conditional rates →

  6. ⑥ Is this contract in a category with high historical revision?

    Repeated adapter-routed requests revise 37% of the time. Request-voiding chains revise 14%. Same disputed sample; same observation; the mechanism path matters.

    When citing, name the category and indicate uncertainty: "Polymarket put the chance at X%, on a contract whose category historically revises ~37% of disputes."

  7. ⑦ How much scoring risk does this category carry?

    Within the binary-binary priced disputed subset (first proposal in {Yes, No}, final payoff in {Yes, No}, at least one trade in the [−30d, 0d) window before settlement), revised contracts are 20.6% of contracts but account for 72% of pooled Brier loss. Confirmed contracts (79.4%) carry only 28% of pooled loss.

    A price on a contract that may revise carries disproportionate scoring risk. Translation for a journalist: a single revised contract degrades a forecaster's pooled accuracy more than three confirmed contracts. Cite the category-level revision rate (Q⑥), not just the price. Source: paper Table 4 & §4.2; n=1,066 priced disputed binary-binary contracts; mean Brier vs final settled outcome.

Suggested citation language

One example for a market with active settlement risk (disputed and revised):

As of {date}, Polymarket implied a {X}% chance that {event}. The contract has been disputed, and similar contracts historically revise from first proposal to final payoff at {Y}%. Readers should treat the price as a market view conditional on the resolution mechanism — not as an unconditional probability.

Adjust to house style. The point is to acknowledge that a quoted price depends on settlement infrastructure, not only on world events.

What this page does not do

  • It does not measure liquidity, depth, or price manipulation. For that, use Bellwether's reportability and cost-to-move metrics.
  • It does not predict settlement outcomes. The risk tier is a screening flag based on rule clarity and historical chain patterns.
  • It does not adjudicate whether a final payoff was correct in real-world terms.
  • For most live, un-disputed markets we do not yet have a per-contract risk profile. The panel currently covers disputed Polymarket contracts (n=2,221).
