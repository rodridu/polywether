How Prediction Markets Resolve: Evidence from Polymarket

Hongzhen Du · Northwestern University · Draft 2026-05-16

Reconstructs the contract-level settlement process for 305,766 resolved Polymarket-UMA contracts. Distinguishes the event that occurs, the written rule that defines what counts, and the settlement label the mechanism produces. Three findings: (1) listing-time contract language predicts later disputes — explicit cancellation language associates with fewer disputes (0.25% vs 0.88%), vague multi-source language with more (1.36% vs 0.39%); (2) conditional on dispute, contract text predicts whether settlement runs through automatic correction (Path D) or jury adjudication (Path C); (3) pooling revised and confirmed contracts distorts forecast scores — revised contracts are ~21% of the priced disputed sample but 30–75% of pooled Brier loss.

Working paper — contribution

A state-contingent contract does not pay directly on a world event. It pays on a final payoff label produced by a settlement institution. Three objects are distinct: the event that occurs, the written rule that defines what counts, and the settlement process that applies the rule and produces the label — the event–rule–label distinction. Disputes arise at the seams between them.

The paper is the first systematic reconstruction of the contract-level settlement paths that produce final payoff labels on Polymarket-UMA. The contribution is descriptive and reduced-form: it documents which listing-time contract terms associate with later dispute and with the enforcement channel used to resolve it, and shows that pooling revised and confirmed contracts distorts forecast-accuracy scores. It does not adjudicate whether any settled label is correct.

Methodology

Polymarket markets and UMA Optimistic Oracle resolution events are ingested into a research database. The disputed-contract sample is the union of contracts with at least one counter-bond posted. Episode-level chains are reconstructed from the UMA RequestPrice / DisputePrice / SettlePrice event sequence.

Sample frame: the disputed-contract panel is the condition-identifier-linked subset of Polymarket markets. The negRisk-umbrella family (high-frequency auto-resolved daily-temperature and per-game sports markets that share one umbrella event across many per-outcome rows) is underrepresented (paper §3). Reports are linked-panel rates throughout; the chain-type and revision analyses below are conditional on the linked settlement-condition panel.

Field schema is in Docs → Static JSON endpoints. The exact COALESCE rules for recovering question text, description, and category from the Polymarket-side metadata are in the paper appendix.

Headline result — contract text and dispute risk

The paper's main evidence (Table 4, N = 305,766 resolved contracts) is that settlement stress is partly predictable from contract language observed at listing, before trading begins. Seven text features are extracted by regex from the question and resolution rules.

Explicit cancellation language is the strongest negative predictor: a 0.25% dispute rate with the feature vs 0.88% without (lift 0.28; within-category logit AME −0.56 pp). Multiple-source / “consensus of credible reporting” language is the strongest positive predictor: 1.36% vs 0.39% (lift 3.50; AME +0.22 pp). The cancellation result survives leave-one-category-out and holds outside sports; it is a reduced-form contract-design screen, not a causal effect of adding a clause.

Conditional on dispute (Table 6, N = 1,480), contract text also predicts the enforcement channel: subjective-event language associates with the automatic-correction channel (Path D), single-named-source disputes with jury adjudication (Path C).

An earlier draft coded rule-implied labels to flag “candidate mismatches.” That diagnostic layer is explicitly excluded from the paper's main analyses (Appendix B.3) because it requires independent validation beyond the paper's scope. The candidate_mismatch flag on the dashboard is a screening signal only, not a paper result; do not cite it as a finding.

What the paper does NOT claim
  • The unconditional rate at which a randomly selected market receives an erroneous final payoff.
  • Whether any specific contract's final payoff is correct in real-world terms.
  • UMA tokenholder voter identity, vote concentration, or turnout.
  • Causal effects of any UMA policy change.
  • Cross-platform generalizability (Kalshi, dYdX, OutcomeFi, etc. not in panel).

Limitations carried into the dashboard
  • Question-text coverage is 79.1%. 1,756 of 2,221 disputed contracts have recoverable question text. The other 465 are visible in the rev3 settlement panel but absent from Polymarket-side metadata.
  • Auditability flags 4–6 (named source / fallback / edge cases) are heuristic regex over a noisy text corpus. They tag whether the relevant language appears, not whether the contract was correctly designed.
  • Settlement-risk tier is a screening flag, not a prediction. It identifies contracts whose payoff depends on discretionary or ambiguous interpretation. It does not predict whether the final payoff is correct.
  • Cases page uses keyword matching to locate publicly-discussed markets in the panel. Three of seven curated cases match the market panel but not the disputed-analysis sample.
