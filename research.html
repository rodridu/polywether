Working paper — contribution

Prediction-market prices are usually interpreted as beliefs about events. But payoffs depend on a contractual mapping from world facts to a settlement label. The paper distinguishes three objects: realized world facts W, the rule-implied label G under the contract's text and source hierarchy, and the settlement state S delivered by the mechanism. It reconstructs this mapping for disputed Polymarket contracts resolved through UMA.

The unit of analysis is a disputed contract. The dependent variable is whether the final payoff state S equals the first proposed outcome P₁. The contribution is descriptive: it documents the mechanism path from P₁ to S, and a candidate-mismatch layer that flags rows where S may not equal G.

Methodology

Polymarket markets and UMA Optimistic Oracle resolution events are ingested into a research database. The disputed-contract sample is the union of contracts with at least one counter-bond posted. Episode-level chains are reconstructed from the UMA RequestPrice / DisputePrice / SettlePrice event sequence.

Sample frame: the disputed-contract panel is the condition-identifier-linked subset of Polymarket markets. The negRisk-umbrella family (high-frequency auto-resolved daily-temperature and per-game sports markets that share one umbrella event across many per-outcome rows) is underrepresented (paper §3). Reports are linked-panel rates throughout; the chain-type and revision analyses below are conditional on the linked settlement-condition panel.

Field schema is in Docs → Static JSON endpoints. The exact COALESCE rules for recovering question text, description, and category from the Polymarket-side metadata are in the paper appendix.

Benchmark transparency — the 64 cases

The candidate-mismatch layer is a diagnostic, not a population error estimate. Two LLM-coded passes from different model families are applied to contract text, named sources, and public evidence on the disputed contracts with a determinate binary rule-implied label. Both passes are blind to the realized settlement state.

The paper headline is 64 cases where both coding passes agree that the contract's settled label diverges from the rule-implied label coded from its own text. Of those 64, 63 sit in Path C (request-voiding chains) and 1 in Path D (repeated-request chains). The Path C concentration is descriptive rather than causal: it shows where candidate mismatches concentrate, not why they arise.

The pipeline also produces a broader pass-1-only count of 94 rows (classification == 'uncorrected_oracle_error') before reconciliation; candidate_mismatches.json exposes all 94 with per-row stratum (55 two_benchmark, 39 frozen), G_i_source, operative_proposed, operative_final, mechanical_final_correct_a1, legacy_final_correct. Use the paper's 64 as the headline; use the broader 94 only when explicitly auditing the pre-reconciliation pass.

Independent blinded re-coding by external coders is the natural next step and is not yet complete (paper §7.3). Treat the 64 as a concrete audit list pending replication. The audit list is distributed in the paper's replication package; see Docs → Benchmark validation transparency for the full schema.

What the paper does NOT claim
  • The unconditional rate at which a randomly selected market receives an erroneous final payoff.
  • Whether any specific contract's final payoff is correct in real-world terms.
  • UMA tokenholder voter identity, vote concentration, or turnout.
  • Causal effects of any UMA policy change.
  • Cross-platform generalizability (Kalshi, dYdX, OutcomeFi, etc. not in panel).

Limitations carried into the dashboard
  • Question-text coverage is 79.1%. 1,756 of 2,221 disputed contracts have recoverable question text. The other 465 are visible in the rev3 settlement panel but absent from Polymarket-side metadata.
  • Auditability flags 4–6 (named source / fallback / edge cases) are heuristic regex over a noisy text corpus. They tag whether the relevant language appears, not whether the contract was correctly designed.
  • Settlement-risk tier is a screening flag, not a prediction. It identifies contracts whose payoff depends on discretionary or ambiguous interpretation. It does not predict whether the final payoff is correct.
  • Cases page uses keyword matching to locate publicly-discussed markets in the panel. Three of seven curated cases match the market panel but not the disputed-analysis sample.
