The paper behind the monitor

The Settlement Risk Monitor is built on the working paper "Settlement-State Construction in Prediction-Market Contracts." This page documents the paper, the sample, the methodology, and what the data does and does not support.

Working paper

"Settlement-State Construction in Prediction-Market Contracts." Hongzhen Du, Northwestern University. Working paper, 2026.

Prediction-market prices are usually interpreted as beliefs about events. But payoffs depend on a contractual mapping from world facts to a settlement label. The paper distinguishes three objects: realized world facts W, the rule-implied label G under the contract's text and source hierarchy, and the settlement state S delivered by the mechanism. It reconstructs this mapping for disputed Polymarket contracts resolved through UMA.

Headline empirical results

  • 661,949 total settlement conditions in the Polymarket-via-UMA panel.
  • 2,221 are disputed (at least one counter-bond posted).
  • 2,197 disputed contracts have an observed final payoff state — the analysis sample.
  • 530 (24.1%) end with a final payoff different from the first proposed outcome.
  • Of those revisions, 352 (66%) occur through repeated adapter-routed requests; 178 (34%) through DVM request-voiding.
  • 94 broad candidate mismatches identified by a two-pass LLM-coded benchmark; 64 agreed across both passes (category-level).

Methodology

Polymarket markets and UMA Optimistic Oracle resolution events are ingested into a research database. The disputed-contract sample is the union of contracts with at least one counter-bond posted. Episode-level chains are reconstructed from the UMA RequestPrice / DisputePrice / SettlePrice event sequence.

Question text comes from a COALESCE over pm_markets.question, episode_panel.question_text, and pm_resolution_events.ancillary_data_decoded. Description (resolution criteria) comes from pm_markets.raw.description. Category from episode_panel.category.

The candidate-mismatch layer uses a two-pass LLM coding protocol against the recoverable contract text and named source. 94 broad candidate mismatches are identified by the first pass; 64 are agreed across both passes (category-level aggregate; per-row agreement is not exposed in this dashboard).

What the paper does NOT claim

  • The unconditional rate at which a randomly selected market receives an erroneous final payoff.
  • Whether any specific contract's final payoff is correct in real-world terms.
  • UMA tokenholder voter identity, vote concentration, or turnout.
  • Causal effects of any UMA policy change.

Limitations carried into the dashboard

  • Question-text coverage is 79.1%. 1,756 of 2,221 disputed contracts have recoverable question text. The other 465 are visible in the rev3 settlement panel but absent from Polymarket-side metadata.
  • Auditability flags 4–6 (named source / fallback / edge cases) are heuristic regex over a noisy text corpus. They tag whether the relevant language appears, not whether the contract was correctly designed.
  • The settlement-risk tier is a screening flag, not a prediction. It identifies contracts whose payoff depends on discretionary or ambiguous interpretation. It does not predict whether the final payoff is correct.
  • Public Case Locator uses keyword matching. Three of seven curated cases match the market panel but not the disputed-analysis sample.
